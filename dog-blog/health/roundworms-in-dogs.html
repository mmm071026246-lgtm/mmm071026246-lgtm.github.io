Roundworms in Dogs: Symptoms, Treatment, and Prevention

Roundworms are one of the most common intestinal parasites in dogs. They can affect puppies and adult dogs, and they may be present even when the dog looks otherwise healthy. Because roundworms can grow quickly and affect nutrient absorption, it is important to recognize the signs early and seek veterinary advice when needed.

Important: If your dog has diarrhea, vomiting, weight loss, or a swollen belly, it is a good idea to speak with a veterinarian about stool testing and deworming.

What roundworms are

Roundworms are parasitic worms that live in the intestines of dogs. They can interfere with digestion and nutrition, especially in younger animals. Some dogs may carry a small number without showing obvious signs, while others may develop more noticeable symptoms as the parasite load increases.

Roundworms are especially concerning in puppies because they can rob the body of nutrients that are needed for growth and development. A puppy with roundworms may appear thin, weak, or less active than expected.

Common signs of roundworms in dogs

  • Pot-bellied appearance, especially in puppies
  • Weight loss or poor growth
  • Vomiting or diarrhea
  • Reduced appetite
  • Dull coat or poor body condition
  • Visible worms in stool or around the rear
  • Fatigue or weakness

These symptoms can also happen with other digestive or parasitic problems, so a veterinarian may recommend testing rather than relying on symptoms alone.

How dogs get roundworms

Roundworms are often passed from mother to puppy, which is one reason puppies are checked so carefully. Dogs may also pick up infection by swallowing eggs from contaminated soil, eating infected material, or coming into contact with contaminated environments.

Because roundworm eggs can survive in the environment for a while, outdoor access and shared spaces can increase exposure. Infected dogs may also spread eggs through feces, which can contaminate the yard or living area.

Why roundworms matter

Roundworms are more than a nuisance. They can cause digestive upset, nutrient loss, and poor growth. In severe cases, especially in young puppies, they can contribute to anemia, dehydration, and other health concerns.

They may also be a concern for people in the household, particularly children, because some roundworm eggs can be spread through contaminated soil or feces. This makes regular hygiene and parasite prevention important.

Diagnosis

A veterinarian may diagnose roundworms by examining a stool sample. Fecal testing can help identify parasite eggs and confirm whether deworming or further treatment is needed. In some cases, the veterinarian may recommend repeat testing after treatment because eggs can be detected after the initial treatment cycle.

If your dog has ongoing symptoms, poor appetite, or a swollen belly, a full exam may be needed to rule out other issues and make sure the treatment plan is appropriate.

Treatment options

Roundworms are usually treated with deworming medication prescribed or recommended by a veterinarian. Depending on the situation, the vet may recommend one dose or a repeat dose a few weeks later to address any parasites that hatch after the first treatment.

It is important to follow the veterinarian?™s instructions carefully, especially for puppies and dogs with severe symptoms. Treatment is often more effective when it is paired with proper sanitation and a plan to prevent reinfection.

Puppies and young dogs

Puppies are especially vulnerable to roundworms. They can become infected before birth or shortly after birth through the mother, so early deworming is often recommended. A puppy that is not growing normally, seems weak, or has a pot-bellied appearance should be examined promptly.

Regular veterinary visits and parasite prevention are especially important for young dogs because their bodies may be less able to tolerate the effects of infection.

How to prevent reinfection

Preventing reinfection involves more than a single treatment. Owners should remove feces from the yard or living area promptly, keep the environment clean, and follow a regular parasite prevention plan recommended by a veterinarian.

  • Pick up dog waste quickly
  • Wash bedding and clean food and water areas regularly
  • Prevent access to contaminated soil or potentially infected animals
  • Use veterinarian-recommended parasite prevention
  • Schedule regular stool checks or exams when advised

Good hygiene and routine care can make a big difference in preventing repeated infections.

When to contact a veterinarian

Contact a veterinarian if your dog has persistent vomiting, diarrhea, weight loss, poor appetite, a swollen belly, or visible worms. Puppies, senior dogs, or dogs that seem weak should be evaluated promptly. A veterinarian can help choose the right treatment and determine whether further testing is needed.
